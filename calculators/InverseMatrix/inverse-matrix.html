Inverse of a Matrix

The inverse of a matrix is a special concept in linear algebra that works much like reciprocals in regular arithmetic. For a number, the reciprocal of a is 1/a — multiplying them gives 1. Similarly, for a square matrix A, its inverse A⁻¹ is the matrix that satisfies: A × A⁻¹ = I Here, I is the identity matrix, the matrix equivalent of the number 1. Not all matrices have inverses — only those that are square (same number of rows and columns) and have a nonzero determinant. Finding the inverse helps solve systems of linear equations, transform geometric objects, and simplify matrix expressions. In practical fields like computer graphics, physics simulations, and engineering, matrix inversion plays a key role in translating, rotating, and scaling objects in space. This section lets you explore how matrix inverses are computed step by step. You can input your own values, calculate the determinant, and watch how each element of the inverse is derived — helping you see not just the result, but the reasoning behind it.
