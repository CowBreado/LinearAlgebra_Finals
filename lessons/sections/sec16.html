Determinant of 3×3

Calculating the determinant of a 3×3 matrix is more involved than the 2×2 case, but it follows a logical pattern that builds on what you already know. The most common method uses expansion by minors (also called cofactor expansion), which breaks down the 3×3 determinant into 2×2 determinants that you already know how to calculate. Once you understand this method, you'll be able to find determinants of any 3×3 matrix.

For a 3×3 matrix:

\( A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{bmatrix} \)

The determinant can be calculated using expansion along the first row:

\( \det(A) = a(ei - fh) - b(di - fg) + c(dh - eg) \)

This formula might look complicated, but it follows a clear pattern. You take each element in the first row (a, b, c), multiply it by the determinant of the 2×2 matrix that remains when you remove that element's row and column, and alternate signs (+, -, +). The 2×2 determinants are called minors.

Here's a step-by-step breakdown. For element a, remove the first row and first column, leaving the 2×2 matrix with entries e, f, h, i. Its determinant is ei - fh. Multiply a by this: a(ei - fh).

For element b, remove the first row and second column, leaving d, f, g, i. Its determinant is di - fg. Multiply b by this and change the sign: -b(di - fg).

For element c, remove the first row and third column, leaving d, e, g, h. Its determinant is dh - eg. Multiply c by this: +c(dh - eg).

Here's a concrete numerical example:

\( \det\left( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} \right) = 1(5×9 - 6×8) - 2(4×9 - 6×7) + 3(4×8 - 5×7) \)

Calculating each part:

\( = 1(45 - 48) - 2(36 - 42) + 3(32 - 35) \)

\( = 1(-3) - 2(-6) + 3(-3) \)

\( = -3 + 12 - 9 = 0 \)

So the determinant is 0, meaning this matrix is not invertible. Notice how we used the 2×2 determinant formula three times, once for each element in the first row.

There's also a method called the Sarrus rule or diagonal method that some people find easier to remember. You write the first two columns again to the right of the matrix, then multiply along diagonals. However, this method only works for 3×3 matrices, while expansion by minors works for any size, so it's worth learning the general method.

Here are some tips for calculating 3×3 determinants:

  • You can expand along any row or column — choose one with zeros if possible to simplify calculations
  • Remember to alternate signs: +, -, + for the first row (or -, +, - if you start with -)
  • The sign pattern follows a checkerboard: the (i,j) position has sign (-1)i+j
  • If a row or column has zeros, expand along that row/column to reduce work
  • Double-check your 2×2 determinant calculations
  • Be careful with negative signs when subtracting

Understanding 3×3 determinants is important because they appear in many applications. In 3D graphics, they're used to determine volumes and orientations. In physics, they appear in cross products and calculating moments. In engineering, they're used in solving systems of three equations. The geometric interpretation is that the absolute value of the determinant represents the volume of a parallelepiped (a 3D shape like a slanted box) formed by the three column vectors.

Practice is essential for mastering 3×3 determinants. Start with simple matrices, work through the calculations step by step, and gradually try more complex examples. Once you're comfortable with 3×3, you'll have the skills to understand determinants of even larger matrices, which use the same expansion method.
